Understanding Burden Rates

A burden rate (or fully-loaded cost) is the true cost of employing someone, including all on-costs beyond their base salary:

• Superannuation — compulsory employer SG contribution (12% FY26) • Payroll Tax — state-levied tax on wages incl. on-cost compounding (varies by state) • Workers Comp — workplace insurance, office/professional rates (varies by state) • Annual Leave — 4 weeks/yr + leave loading (~8.7-9.3% of salary) • Sick/Personal Leave — 10 days/yr accrual (~2.9-3.0% of salary) • Public Holidays — effective impact incl. replacement costs (~5.2-5.9%)
Permanent employees carry full burden (~35-38% uplift). Casual/PAYG workers carry super + payroll tax + workers comp (~18-20%). RCTI Contractors have zero burden — their daily rate is the total cost.
Formula: Hourly Rate = Base Salary × (1 + Total Burden%) ÷ Annual Hours | All salary inputs are base (excl. super). Standard: 2,080hrs (40hrs/wk); WA: 1,950hrs (37.5hrs/wk). Rates per company Burden Calculator v0.8.

How to read this table

The hourly rate columns ($80k–$220k) show the fully-loaded cost per hour at each base salary level, after applying all burden components. Use these to compare what a resource actually costs the project.

The Max Base Salary column works backwards from the selected project’s financials to find the highest base salary you could offer a new hire while keeping the project at the target gross margin (set per-project in Configuration → Project Editor, default 25%). It is calculated as:

remaining budget = revenue × (100% − target margin%) − current total cost
max salary = remaining budget ÷ (project weeks × FTE ÷ annual hours) ÷ (1 + burden%)

FTE changes the result because it controls how many full-time equivalents share the remaining budget. At 1.0 FTE the full budget goes to one person (higher max salary). At 2.0 FTE the same budget is split across two people (lower max salary each). Use this to evaluate trade-offs — e.g. one senior at $180k vs two juniors at $90k.

Configure the burden rate components used to calculate fully-loaded hourly costs. All salary inputs throughout the tool represent base salary (excl. super). The burden rate is applied on top to derive the true cost of employment.
How burden rates work

A burden rate captures the total cost of employment beyond base salary. For a permanent employee, this typically adds 35–38% on top of base salary. The components are:

• Superannuation (SG) — compulsory employer contribution, set by legislation per financial year • Payroll Tax — state-levied tax on total wages including super (rate varies by state; effective rate shown includes on-cost compounding) • Workers Compensation — workplace insurance premium; rates shown are for office/professional services, not state-wide averages • Annual Leave — 4 weeks per year plus 17.5% leave loading where applicable (~8.7–9.3% of base) • Sick/Personal Leave — 10 days per year accrual-based cost (~2.9–3.0% of base) • Public Holidays — effective cost including replacement/productivity impact (~5.2–5.9% of base)
Three resource types: Permanent = all components (super + payroll tax + workers comp + leave + holidays) | Casual/PAYG = super + payroll tax + workers comp only (no leave entitlements) | RCTI Contractor = zero burden (daily rate is total cost). RCTI stands for Recipient Created Tax Invoice — the hiring company issues the invoice on behalf of the contractor, common for independent contractors where the business (recipient) creates the tax invoice rather than the supplier.
Default rates are from the Burden Calculator effective 16 Feb 2026 with super at 12% (FY26). All state rates are verified; NT is interpolated from NSW.
